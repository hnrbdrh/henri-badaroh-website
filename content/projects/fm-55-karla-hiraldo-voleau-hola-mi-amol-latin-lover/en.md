![*Fig 1.* Foam Magazine #55, p. 33](/projects/foam-magazine-55-talent/images/fm55-image-1.jpg)
A woman who shoots men. In Karla Hiraldo Voleau's body of work, men are carefully observed. Their strong bodies— tan, brown, and black-contemplated and lingered over. One could easily perceive them as  _machos_, stereotypical men from the Latin parts of the American continent. They could also just be sons, fathers, or lovers, accustomed to continuously performing their taught masculine roles. Voleau allows them to take her to places, from deserted beaches to motel rooms. Maybe their own bed at home. They take her to those places and there she shoots them. Delicately, lustfully, femininely. Yes, her photographs are ingrained with femi-ninity, with a gaze that does not make the men fragile, yet reveals their vulnerability.

Voleau grew up in France and recently completed an MA at ECAL, in Switzerland. Infused with references from Sophie Calle to Viviane Sassen, Voleau's projects are intrinsically personal and performative. For  _Hola Mi Amol_, co-published by Self Publish Be Happy and ECAL, she travelled to the Dominican Republic in search of self-understanding and, ultimately, love. Born from the relationship of a French woman and a Dominican man, her upbringing in a mixed family was rooted in legends about the simultaneous appeal and danger of _Quisqueyan_ — Dominican — men. The conflicting opinions within Voleau's family on the objectionable men only fuelled her with daring curiosity, culminating in a trip to the Caribbean.

In a way, the project became the material for Voleau to learn about the Dominican Republic as a whole. Following in the footsteps of the French women in her family who, in one way or another, all had had significant experiences on the island, Voleau sought to connect with the men who had been frantically kept from her, as if to re-enact her parents' rendezvous. Driven into playing the role of a tourist, Voleau's photographs, on the most superficial level, resemble snapshots of a white-passing European woman exploring a foreign land and its male population — cruising. But instead of an account or a critique of sex tourism, she presents a quasi-ethnographic study of Dominican men, skilfully balancing an alternative gaze on the hypersexualised bodies of black and Hispanic men, and an unusual take on the subject-object axis through her encounters with seven strangers.

The men of  _Hola Mi Amol_  are seen performing their roles within the patriarchal system of Latin America, which reinforces heteronormative behaviour and normalises women as objects — pearls waiting to be collected from the seabed, at best. We observe the ways they try to lure Voleau, with their pick-up lines and tawdry charm. Soon it dawns on viewers that Voleau is wittily allowing them to play a game they might indeed, for a moment or two, think they are in control of. In fact, she is the one exposing these relationships' dynamics, constantly redirecting the narrative and dismantling how they perform their masculinities. As she fluidly transitions from iPhone snapshots to classic film portraiture, from selfies to GoPro underwater shots, we can't help but wonder about the storyline behind her portraits. This is likely where Voleau's power lies: venturing through the narrative we mentally fill in the gaps of her story, ponder what did or will take place, ultimately realising that through seemingly innocent images, she challenges the viewer's presumptions, expectations, and behaviours.

Proceeding from  _Hola Mi Amol_'s journey and as an attempt to distance herself from the foreground of her work, Voleau's follow-up project  _Latin Lover_  shifts focus to the Dominican men in New York who seek to depart from the old, preconceived ideas of how Latin men are, and behave.

Voleau broadens the conversation by trying to decipher how growing up in a different country, under a different culture deeply affects men's understandings of their own identity, and ultimately, masculinity.

To define Voleau's work as simply a deconstruction of masculinity overlooks the strong performative elements of her projects. By subjecting herself to the camera lens, she is consciously instigating a gaze that firmly reminds us who is in charge. In Voleau's photographs, we might sense the unholy trinity of sand, sex and sun on men's bodies as she caresses them with her camera. However, unlike a male gaze that depicts women as objects of desire, she is not trying to objectify anyone, 'to expect that my looking at men equates with how men traditionally look at women is curious, to say the least, Voleau explains. Many of her photographs are actually unexpected self-portraits in which the understanding of what is fact and what is fiction becomes blurred by the relationship between photographer, subject, and self. This happens, for instance, when she lowers her guard after falling in love with one of the characters, revealing the risks of the artist-tourist dichotomy.

The only thing that is certain about this body of work is the forthright message that every single image displays a woman in control of what and how she wants us to see, and above all, in control of directing her own past and present. The woman with the camera holds the power.
