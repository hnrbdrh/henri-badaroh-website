Bowei Yang is a memory (re)collector. In a dream-like, cinematic style, photographs of an endless autumn are woven together by nostalgia, feelings of inadequacy, and a reality carefully assembled. His work chronicles the troubled lives of young people facing the dilemmas of belonging and the prospects of becoming.

Yang graduated from the Beijing Film Academy and is currently earning an MA at the Royal College of Art in England. Born in southern China, and raised in a conservative family of Christian faith, his projects are intrinsically connected to the struggles faced in an alienating environment while being far from home, and the possibility of a full understanding of his truer, queerer self. Working predominantly in his birthplace, Yang delves into the complications that affect the local youth as a reflection on more universal issues: 'I am happy to show how those living in China have developed tremendously in terms of material resources while there is still room for improvement when it comes to people's spiritual needs', he tells me. The world, with all its ever-developing technologies, seems to have lost sight of collective allyship in a model that keeps pushing us to absolute self-centrism. This is particularly endemic amongst the LGBTQ+ demographics who, in essentially heteronormative nations like China, lack basic support from both family and society.

In _If Spring Could Feel Ache_, we see indeed soul-searching portraits of the young Chinese gay community melded with glimpses of urban life. These photos, however, do not come unscathed — they present a city in unpeaceful coexistence with nature: butterflies trapped in a container, the petals of a torn rose pierced with needles, stones tied together. Images of 'seized' nature and introspective portraits of youngsters lost in the fog of their thoughts translate well how suffocating and isolating life is, when stripped of its full potential. Linked by repressive forces, nature and people craft together a full-bodied sense of captivity, of vulnerability. A single, compelling image of the pains of being exposed to a world not entirely prepared to accommodate them, just like flowers growing through concrete.

Drawing from literary works heavy on psychology, like those of French Alain Robbe-Grillet and Chinese classic author Cáo Xuěqín, Yang intuitively juxtaposes fantasy and memory so as to unveil new facets of his identity and sexuality. The compositions show a natural world that is never undisturbed; and like the reminiscence of a dream or even the retelling of childhood memories, reality is bent, malleable, independent of an absolute truth. The well-nigh forensic exercise of repeatedly capturing and reimagining a scene, a place, a memory, the lives of others, make Yang's use of photography not only a source of documentation of ostracised lives but also an instrument through which he manages to render his own self. Documentary photography as a tender portrayal of teen queerness in contemporary China, but also a window into Yang's psyche: a reflexive self-portrait.

After coming out to his parents, Yang was subjected to psychological intervention which propelled a slow process of trauma sedimentation. In his tinted, bloodstained portraiture, it becomes evident how, deep down, past a seemingly unbroken figure, there can be wounds invisible to the naked eye. In a way, what we see throughout this body of work is a more unusual, less rebellious take on the experience of existing uninvitedly, of teenage angst: a looming sense of melancholy floats about. In the murky sea of teenage life, between the feelings of being asphyxiated and held back or unable to fully morph into oneself yet, that is what seems to be at its core: melancholy. Sometimes sorrow shapeshifts into rage and solitude, but in the end, it comes down to melancholy.

However, not everything is doom and gloom in _If Spring Could Feel Ache_. Personally, after gazing at Bowei Yang's images, the sentiment that has dawned on me can be described by the words of Vietnamese-Unitedstatian poet Ocean Vuong:

><em>the body is a blade that sharpens by cutting.</em>

There is a poetic resilience embedded in the experience of existing uninvitedly, of continuing despite the hardships.
