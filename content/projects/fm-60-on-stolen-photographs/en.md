On my most recent trip to Brazil, I decided to start collecting my extended family's documents and photos as another lap within my long-term project of trying to learn about my heritage I have been working on this for the past ten years, trying to build my family tree and track down my relatives to the best of my ability. This is not an easy task, as it is also the reality of many other Brazilian families, particularly those families of Black and/or poor origins, for whom slavery and colonialism destroyed the possibility of reliable documentation — but that is another story.

While digging into the archives of cousins, aunts, uncles and grandparents' siblings, I have encountered, in my aunt's possession, a few photographs that immediately drew my attention: prints featuring my parents at a younger age and some that even included my sister and me. To my surprise, I had never seen those images. On the back of a particular photograph of the four of us, printed on Kodak standard paper, I have even found (in my mother's distinctive handwriting) the following message: 'Sé Square, São Paulo — in front of the cathedral. At the bottom, a subtle 'to [my sister] Abigail. It turns out that these photos were not with the said sister. Rather, they were found under guard of my father's sister instead, by the name of Nice, much southwards — on the other side of the country. When I discreetly quizzed her on why this photo was there, she, visibly embarrassed, replied: 'your mother gave it to me'.

I did not believe her for a moment.

And I was not bothered, either. My mum would probably have furtively stolen it back had she seen it there, I bet she would. I, for one, understood it as a little secret that occurs amongst Brazilian families for a long, long time. The illegal yet inoffensive act of stealing photographs so they can be added to the culprit's archive has almost turned into a tradition.
The silent, mutual understanding that comes when one sees their formerly owned, now stolen image in a frame or an album in the thief's house — either because the misdemeanour was forgotten or by sheer sloppiness — is also part of the experience.

I had many questions regarding the entire process. Why steal, why not ask for a copy? What is the motivation behind it... impatience, control, love? An undiagnosed kleptomania? In today's context, it must be reminded here that printing is a dying phenomenon within families. The advent of Mark Zuckerberg's evil triad of Facebook, Instagram and WhatsApp is gradually strangling both the quality and physicality of familial photography. However, even decades ago, it was not easy nor cheap to produce a good photo print, let alone the risk of losing the original forever with back-and-forth correspondences between long-distance relatives. I am fully aware I might be nodding to the stealing of photographs, and that is a correct read —there is amusement in such a behaviour, to say the least.

Is it not the point for photographs' existence — besides its obvious historical and commemorative functions — the circulation of images? Those are goods and proofs of our existence and they only achieve their purpose and potential by sharing with others.

My aunt Abigail probably would have been happy with that photo had she received it back in the late 1990s. Presumably it would have made her happy, it would have possibly strengthened the sisterly bond between my mother and her; but knowing her as I do, I doubt this photo would still be reachable and in good conditions by now. Owing to my aunt Nice's little mischievous act, I managed to see such a beautiful and essential moment of my nuclear family. In this case, the act of stealing denoted a strong desire to keep a memory safe and sound, rescued to be timelessly preserved for future generations, or just for a nostalgic onlooker like me.
